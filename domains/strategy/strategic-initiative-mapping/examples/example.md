# Strategic Initiative Mapping — Example

## Scenario
A $180M industrial services company has completed a 3-year strategy refresh organized around four strategic themes: digital operations, operational excellence, geographic expansion and new service lines. The strategy cycle generated 22 candidate initiatives across all themes. The available budget envelope is $15M over three years with a peak annual capacity of approximately $6M. Leadership requires a structured, transparent prioritization to determine which initiatives enter the active portfolio and in what sequence.

## Inputs
- **Strategic objectives:** Four themes with priority weighting — Digital Operations (35%), Operational Excellence (30%), Geographic Expansion (20%), New Services (15%)
- **Candidate initiatives:** 22 initiatives ranging from ERP upgrade ($4.2M) to field technician certification program ($0.4M)
- **Resource envelope:** $15M total, 3-year horizon, max 8 FTE dedicated program staff
- **Existing commitments:** Legacy system maintenance contract consuming $1.1M/year; two in-flight initiatives already consuming $1.8M in Year 1

## Analysis

**Step 1 — Objective review and weighting confirmation:**
Leadership validates the four strategic themes and confirms Digital Operations as the highest-weighted pillar given the board mandate on productivity improvement. The 35/30/20/15 weighting is locked as the scoring anchor for the initiative evaluation.

**Step 2 — Candidate initiative compilation:**
All 22 initiatives are consolidated from business unit submissions, the strategy offsite output and a functional review. Three duplicates are identified (two separate "mobile workforce" proposals and overlapping customer portal concepts) and collapsed to one each, reducing the working list to 19 unique candidates.

**Step 3 — Scoring on impact and feasibility:**
Each initiative is scored 1–5 on strategic impact (weighted alignment to the four objectives) and 1–5 on feasibility (inverse of cost, complexity and delivery time). The ERP consolidation scores 4.8 on impact (Digital Operations alignment) but 2.1 on feasibility due to $4.2M cost and 30-month implementation. The technician certification program scores 3.9 on impact and 4.6 on feasibility at $0.4M with a 6-month cycle time.

**Step 4 — Impact-feasibility matrix plotting:**
Quadrant distribution: 6 initiatives in high impact/high feasibility (proceed); 5 in high impact/low feasibility (phase or pre-enable); 5 in low impact/high feasibility (optional); 3 in low impact/low feasibility (decline). The ERP consolidation falls in high impact/low feasibility and is phased to Year 2 pending a Year 1 data architecture enabler.

**Step 5 — Dependency mapping:**
The data architecture initiative (Year 1, $0.9M) is identified as an enabler for three downstream Digital Operations initiatives including the ERP consolidation. The geographic expansion initiative into the Southwest region requires completion of the field operations playbook (Operational Excellence theme). Two initiatives in New Services are dependent on the CRM upgrade completing first.

**Step 6 — Resource envelope application:**
Applying the $15M envelope and dependency sequencing produces: 11 active initiatives (Year 1: 4 initiatives, $4.8M; Year 2: 4 initiatives, $5.9M; Year 3: 3 initiatives, $4.3M). Six initiatives are placed in reserve with defined activation triggers — primarily contingent on Year 1 budget performance and the feasibility of the ERP consolidation proceeding on schedule.

**Step 7 — Sponsor assignment and metrics:**
Each of the 11 active initiatives is assigned a C-suite sponsor and a delivery owner. Success metrics are defined: ERP consolidation targets a 22% reduction in back-office labor cost; technician certification targets 15% reduction in first-visit failure rate. The portfolio roadmap document is signed off by the Executive Committee.
