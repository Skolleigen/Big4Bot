# Balanced Scorecard

## Domain
Strategy

## Description
Strategic performance management framework that translates organizational strategy into measurable objectives across four perspectives: financial, customer, internal processes, and learning and growth. A strategy map establishes causal linkages between objectives across perspectives, making the logic of strategy explicit and testable.

## Purpose
Most organizations manage performance through financial metrics alone, which are lagging indicators that report outcomes without explaining their causes or guiding corrective action. The Balanced Scorecard addresses this by creating a multi-perspective measurement system tied to a causal model of how strategy creates value, ensuring that leading operational and capability indicators are managed alongside financial results. It also provides a structured vehicle for communicating strategy across the organization and aligning resource allocation to strategic priorities.

## When to Use
- When translating a newly formed or revised organizational strategy into operational targets and performance measures
- When management lacks visibility into whether day-to-day operations are creating or eroding strategic value
- When there is a need to align business units, departments, and individual roles to enterprise-level strategic objectives
- When existing KPI systems are disconnected from strategy or dominated by financial lag measures that do not enable timely course correction

## When NOT to Use
- In organizations that have not yet articulated a clear strategy — the scorecard translates strategy; it does not create it
- For operational performance dashboards where the purpose is real-time monitoring rather than strategic management
- In early-stage organizations where strategy changes frequently enough that stabilizing a scorecard would require continuous redesign

## Inputs Required
- Documented organizational strategy, mission, and vision statement
- Financial performance data and targets (revenue, margin, cost, return metrics)
- Customer performance data (satisfaction scores, retention rates, market share, NPS)
- Internal process performance data (cycle times, quality metrics, throughput rates)
- Current learning and growth indicators (employee engagement, capability gaps, technology maturity)

## Optional Inputs
- Competitive benchmarking data for target-setting calibration
- Prior balanced scorecard or strategy map from previous planning cycle

## Diagnostic Process
1. Clarify organizational strategy and vision by confirming the strategic choices already made (where to play, how to win) before defining measurement; do not use the scorecard exercise to make undecided strategic choices.
2. Define two to five strategic objectives per perspective — financial, customer, internal processes, and learning and growth — ensuring each objective is specific enough to be measured and is directly connected to the strategy.
3. Identify one to three KPIs for each objective that measure progress toward that objective; distinguish between lag measures (outcomes) and lead measures (drivers); require both types where possible.
4. Set quantitative targets for each KPI covering the planning horizon, calibrated against baseline performance, competitive benchmarks, and strategic ambition; targets must be specific and time-bound.
5. Define strategic initiatives — the specific projects and programs that will enable the organization to achieve each objective; map each initiative to the objectives it supports.
6. Build the strategy map by drawing causal linkage arrows between objectives across perspectives, from learning and growth upward through internal processes and customer to financial; validate that the causal logic is coherent and testable.
7. Assign ownership for each objective and initiative to a named executive or function; establish review cadence and escalation protocol for off-track metrics.

## Output
The analysis produces a fully populated balanced scorecard with objectives, KPIs, targets, and initiative assignments across four perspectives, accompanied by a strategy map showing causal linkages between objectives. The output is designed to serve as the primary strategic management tool for executive review and business unit alignment.

## References

- Robert S. Kaplan and David P. Norton: *The Strategy-Focused Organization* (Harvard Business School Press, 2001) — Extended the scorecard into an enterprise-wide management system with strategy maps, cascading, and strategic alignment processes
