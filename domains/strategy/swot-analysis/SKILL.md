# SWOT Analysis

## Domain
Strategy

## Description
Strategic diagnostic framework that maps internal strengths and weaknesses against external opportunities and threats to inform strategic decision-making. It produces a structured inventory of factors that enables the generation of four categories of cross-quadrant strategic options.

## Purpose
Organizations frequently lack a structured mechanism to reconcile internal capability assessments with external market conditions before committing to strategy. SWOT analysis creates a shared diagnostic baseline that surfaces hidden vulnerabilities, unexploited opportunities, and strategic conflicts early in a planning cycle. The framework also enables cross-functional alignment by grounding strategic conversations in evidence rather than opinion.

## When to Use
- At the outset of a strategic planning cycle to establish situational awareness before defining objectives
- Before entering a new market, launching a product, or making a major capital allocation decision
- When evaluating a merger, acquisition, or partnership target
- When organizational performance has materially deviated from plan and root-cause diagnosis is needed

## When NOT to Use
- As a substitute for deep quantitative analysis when financial or operational decisions require precise modeling
- When the organization lacks access to reliable internal performance data or credible external market intelligence
- When the output will not be connected to a specific decision or action — standalone analyses without follow-through produce no value

## Inputs Required
- Internal performance data (financial results, operational KPIs, customer metrics)
- Organizational capability inventory (people, technology, processes, assets)
- External market and competitive intelligence (industry trends, competitor profiles, regulatory landscape)
- Stakeholder input representing multiple functional perspectives (interviews, surveys, or workshop outputs)

## Optional Inputs
- Prior strategic plans and their performance against targets
- Benchmarking data against industry peers or best-in-class operators

## Diagnostic Process
1. Gather and validate internal performance data across financial, operational, and customer dimensions; establish a factual baseline before soliciting qualitative input.
2. Identify organizational strengths by examining capabilities, assets, and performance metrics that demonstrably exceed competitive benchmarks or internal targets; require supporting evidence for each item listed.
3. Identify organizational weaknesses by examining capability gaps, underperforming KPIs, resource constraints, and structural limitations relative to strategic requirements; be specific about magnitude and root cause.
4. Scan the external environment for opportunities by analyzing market trends, unmet customer needs, regulatory changes, and competitor vulnerabilities that the organization could realistically exploit within the planning horizon.
5. Identify external threats by assessing competitive dynamics, macroeconomic risks, regulatory headwinds, and technological disruptions that could erode current performance or block strategic objectives.
6. Map interactions between quadrants to generate four categories of strategic options: SO (deploy strengths to capture opportunities), ST (deploy strengths to mitigate threats), WO (address weaknesses to unlock opportunities), WT (minimize weaknesses to avoid compounding threats).
7. Evaluate and prioritize strategic options by assessing each option's potential impact and the organization's realistic capacity to execute; eliminate options not actionable within the planning horizon.
8. Document prioritized findings and connect each strategic option to a subsequent planning artifact such as a strategic initiative, balanced scorecard objective, or OKR.

## Output
The analysis produces a populated SWOT matrix, a set of cross-quadrant strategic options across SO, ST, WO, and WT categories, and a prioritized list of recommended actions with supporting rationale. The output is designed to serve as direct input into strategic initiative mapping, balanced scorecard design, or OKR development.

## References
- Albert Humphrey (SRI International, 1960s): Original SWOT framework developed during Stanford Research Institute project analyzing Fortune 500 corporate planning failures
- Heinz Weihrich: *The TOWS Matrix — A Tool for Situational Analysis* (1982) — Extended SWOT into a cross-impact matrix generating four actionable categories of strategic options
- Michael E. Porter: *Competitive Strategy* (1980) — Porter's Five Forces provides a structured external threat and opportunity scan that complements the SWOT external quadrants
