# Lean Startup Cycle

## Domain
Product & Innovation

## Description
Iterative product development methodology using Build-Measure-Learn loops to test hypotheses with minimum viable products, reducing waste and accelerating validated learning before scaling investment.

## Purpose
The Lean Startup Cycle addresses the core risk in product development: investing significant resources in building products based on untested assumptions. By decomposing the business model into explicit hypotheses and testing the riskiest ones first with the smallest possible experiment, teams generate evidence before committing to full development. The methodology creates a structured decision framework — pivot or persevere — grounded in measured outcomes rather than intuition.

## When to Use
- A product or feature is being built on assumptions that have not been empirically validated
- The team is debating whether to continue investment in a product direction without clear market signal
- A new market, customer segment, or distribution channel is being entered for the first time
- Leadership needs a structured framework for managing a portfolio of product bets with defined learning milestones

## When NOT to Use
- The product is in a mature market with well-understood customer behavior and the problem is execution quality, not hypothesis validation
- Regulatory requirements mandate full-specification development before any user exposure
- The MVP concept cannot be meaningfully constructed within the available time and budget constraints
- The organization's risk tolerance or brand position prohibits exposure of early-stage product to customers

## Inputs Required
- Statement of the core business hypotheses underlying the product or feature
- Definition of the target customer segment for initial testing
- Proposed success metrics and measurement approach
- Minimum viable product concept or experiment design

## Optional Inputs
- Existing customer behavioral data that can reduce the number of hypotheses requiring active testing
- Competitive benchmarks to calibrate what "validated" looks like for key metrics

## Diagnostic Process
1. Identify all key business hypotheses embedded in the product or initiative, making each assumption explicit and falsifiable (e.g., "customers will pay $X for feature Y" rather than "customers want feature Y").
2. Rank hypotheses by risk: identify the single riskiest assumption — the one whose failure would invalidate the entire business case — and designate it as the first test target.
3. Design the minimum viable product or experiment that will produce sufficient evidence to confirm or refute the riskiest hypothesis, using the least time and resource expenditure possible.
4. Define success metrics and pass/fail thresholds before building, ensuring the measurement criteria are agreed upon and cannot be revised after observing results.
5. Build the MVP and release it to a defined subset of the target customer segment, instrumenting the experience to capture the designated metrics accurately.
6. Measure actual outcomes against the pre-defined hypothesis and thresholds; collect both quantitative metrics and qualitative feedback to explain the numbers.
7. Convene a pivot-or-persevere decision: if the hypothesis is validated, proceed to the next riskiest assumption; if invalidated, identify which element of the model must change (customer segment, problem framing, solution approach, or pricing) and formulate a revised hypothesis for the next cycle.

## Output
The output is a documented experiment log recording the hypothesis tested, MVP design, measured results, and the pivot-or-persevere decision with supporting rationale. Across multiple cycles, this produces an evidence base that de-risks subsequent investment decisions and informs roadmap prioritization with validated data rather than assumptions.
