# Design Thinking — Example

## Scenario
A regional hospital network is experiencing consistently low patient satisfaction scores related to the discharge process. Patients report feeling unprepared after leaving, and the care coordination team suspects the root cause is unclear discharge communication rather than clinical quality. A design thinking engagement has been commissioned to understand the actual patient and staff experience before any technology or process intervention is designed.

## Inputs
- **Innovation challenge:** Improve the patient discharge experience to reduce avoidable 30-day readmissions and increase post-discharge self-management confidence
- **Target users:** Adult inpatients being discharged to home after a 2+ day stay, and the nursing and case management staff responsible for executing discharge
- **Existing context:** Patient satisfaction scores for "discharge information" average 62nd percentile nationally; 30-day readmission rate is 14.3%, above the 12.1% national average; no prior user research has been conducted on this process

## Analysis

1. **Empathize:** Conducted 11 patient interviews within 48 hours of discharge, observing two full discharge workflows from medication reconciliation through physical departure. Interviewed 6 nurses and 3 case managers about their discharge process experience. Key observations: patients received an average of 7 separate documents during discharge; nurses reported spending 40% of discharge time managing paperwork rather than answering patient questions; patients described feeling "information overload" rather than informed.

2. **Define:** Synthesized observations using affinity mapping into four insight clusters: information volume vs. information clarity, timing mismatch (discharge preparation begins too late in the stay), role confusion (patients do not know who to call with post-discharge questions), and absent caregiver inclusion (family members who will support recovery are rarely present or briefed during discharge). Problem statement: "Patients preparing to go home need a clear, personalized summary of what to do next and who to call — not a collection of generic documents — because the current process creates confusion rather than confidence at the most vulnerable transition point in their care."

3. **Ideate:** Facilitated a 90-minute ideation session with a cross-functional team of 2 nurses, 1 case manager, 1 patient experience liaison, and 1 physician. Generated 34 solution concepts. Top-voted clusters after dot voting: (a) a single-page personalized "What Matters Most" discharge card summarizing the 3 most important actions for that specific patient, (b) a 48-hour post-discharge phone check-in protocol from a dedicated nurse navigator, (c) a bedside teach-back session initiated 12 hours before discharge rather than at departure, and (d) a QR-code linked video that patients access at home to review instructions.

4. **Prototype:** Built three prototypes in paper form: (a) a draft "What Matters Most" card template filled in with a sample patient scenario, (b) a role-play script for the 48-hour phone check-in call, and (c) a storyboard depicting the earlier teach-back session flow. Each prototype was designed to test a different assumption: (a) whether simplified summary content is preferred over complete documents, (b) whether patients would answer and value a follow-up call, and (c) whether earlier teach-back improves comprehension before the stress of imminent discharge.

5. **Test:** Tested all three prototypes with 8 recently discharged patients and 4 nurses across two sessions. Results: the "What Matters Most" card received strongly positive reactions — 7 of 8 patients said they would have found it more useful than the documents they received. The phone check-in concept was well-received but raised a feasibility concern: patients expected calls from their own care team, not a generic navigator. Earlier teach-back was rated as the most operationally difficult to implement but the highest-value concept by nursing staff.

6. **Iterate:** Revised the phone check-in prototype to use a warm hand-off from the discharging nurse introducing the navigator by name before discharge. Retested the revised concept with 4 patients; acceptance increased and the "stranger concern" was eliminated. The "What Matters Most" card was refined to a fill-in template with 5 fixed fields completed by the nurse during the teach-back session, linking the two highest-rated concepts into a single integrated intervention.
