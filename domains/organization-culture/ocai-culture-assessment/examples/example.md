# OCAI Culture Assessment — Example

## Scenario
A 900-person manufacturing company producing industrial components has recently completed a strategic planning cycle that emphasizes greater cross-functional collaboration and increased product innovation as responses to competitive pressure. The Chief HR Officer commissions an OCAI assessment to establish a baseline culture measurement and identify the specific cultural gaps relative to the strategy's requirements. The survey is administered to 340 employees across all levels and four manufacturing plants.

## Inputs
- **Organization:** 900-person industrial components manufacturer, four plants
- **Respondents:** 340 employees; 78% response rate; segmented by plant and by level (executive, plant management, frontline supervisors, operators)
- **Current OCAI scores:** Hierarchy = 42, Market = 28, Clan = 18, Adhocracy = 12 (total = 100)
- **Preferred OCAI scores:** Hierarchy = 28, Market = 25, Clan = 27, Adhocracy = 20 (total = 100)
- **Strategic drivers:** Increase cross-functional product development collaboration; reduce siloed decision-making between plants; launch 2 new product lines per year
- **Primary concern:** Culture is perceived as too internally focused and process-rigid to support the innovation and collaboration ambitions

## Analysis

**Step 1 — OCAI survey administration:**
The standard 6-dimension OCAI instrument is administered via the company's employee survey platform over a 10-day window. The forced-choice scoring method (100 points distributed across four quadrant options per dimension) is explained in a 5-minute video briefing from the CHRO to maximize response quality. The survey is translated into the three languages spoken across the four plants. Response rate of 78% (340 of 436 invited employees) meets the minimum threshold for reliable reporting at the plant level (minimum: 20 respondents per unit).

**Step 2 — Response collection and aggregation:**
Responses are aggregated across all six dimensions by quadrant. Dimension-level results reveal important variation: the Hierarchy concentration is most pronounced in the "Management of Employees" dimension (Hierarchy = 51) and "Organizational Glue" dimension (Hierarchy = 48), reflecting strong top-down management norms and procedural cohesion as the primary binding mechanism. Adhocracy scores lowest on "Dominant Characteristics" (Adhocracy = 8) and "Criteria of Success" (Adhocracy = 10), indicating that innovation and creativity are not currently recognized as success markers in the day-to-day organizational culture.

**Step 3 — Current and preferred profile aggregation:**
Overall current culture profile: Hierarchy = 42, Market = 28, Clan = 18, Adhocracy = 12. Overall preferred culture profile: Hierarchy = 28, Market = 25, Clan = 27, Adhocracy = 20. Both profiles are plotted as radar charts. The current profile shows a pronounced Hierarchy dominance with a compressed Adhocracy quadrant. The preferred profile shows a significant flattening of the radar — employees collectively desire a more balanced culture — with Clan showing the largest intended shift and Hierarchy the largest intended reduction.

**Step 4 — Current culture radar chart:**
The current culture radar chart shows a skewed profile strongly anchored in Hierarchy (42) and Market (28), with Clan (18) and Adhocracy (12) notably compressed. Plant-level segmentation reveals that Plant 3 (the newest plant, opened 4 years ago) scores lower Hierarchy (36) and higher Adhocracy (17) than the company average, suggesting a nascent cultural variation that could serve as an internal model for Adhocracy development. Executive-level respondents score Adhocracy 4 points higher than frontline supervisors, indicating a perception gap in how much innovation is currently valued and enabled across levels.

**Step 5 — Preferred culture radar chart:**
The preferred culture radar chart shows employees desire: Hierarchy to decrease from 42 to 28 (-14 points), Market to decrease marginally from 28 to 25 (-3 points), Clan to increase from 18 to 27 (+9 points), and Adhocracy to increase from 12 to 20 (+8 points). The preferred profile is significantly more balanced than the current one. The Clan and Adhocracy increases are the most strategically aligned with the cross-functional collaboration and innovation objectives, confirming that the strategic ambition and employees' cultural preferences are directionally consistent.

**Step 6 — Gap analysis by quadrant and dimension:**
Quadrant gaps ranked by magnitude: (1) Clan gap: +9 points — the largest positive gap; driven primarily by "Management of Employees" (+12) and "Organizational Glue" (+11) dimensions, indicating that employees want significantly more people-centered leadership and cohesion through values and relationships rather than procedures. (2) Adhocracy gap: +8 points — second largest; driven primarily by "Criteria of Success" (+10) and "Dominant Characteristics" (+9), indicating employees want innovation to become a recognized success measure. (3) Hierarchy gap: -14 points — the largest reduction required; driven by "Management of Employees" (-16) and "Organizational Glue" (-13), confirming that current management style and the procedural binding mechanism are the primary sources of the over-Hierarchy dynamic. (4) Market gap: -3 points — the smallest gap; the current external competitive and results orientation is broadly appropriate, requiring only minor moderation.

**Step 7 — Targeted interventions per gap area:**
Three intervention streams are prioritized: (1) Clan gap interventions: Introduce cross-plant project teams with shared objectives and joint recognition; redesign the management capability program to add coaching and mentoring skills alongside technical management; establish quarterly cross-plant "communities of practice" for engineering and quality functions. (2) Adhocracy gap interventions: Create a quarterly innovation fund ($500K) available to cross-plant teams proposing new product concepts with a 90-day proof-of-concept cycle; add "innovation contribution" as a 10% weighted criterion in the annual performance review; celebrate one internal innovation success story per month in company communications. (3) Hierarchy reduction interventions: Reduce the mandatory sign-off layers for operational decisions under $100K from 4 to 2 levels; shift the management development program emphasis from control and compliance skills toward enabling and empowering skills; redesign the "Organizational Glue" mechanisms to add values-based recognition programs alongside the current procedural compliance incentives.
