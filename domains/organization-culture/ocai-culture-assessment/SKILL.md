# OCAI Culture Assessment

## Domain
Organization & Culture

## Description
Validated psychometric instrument based on the Competing Values Framework that measures organizational culture type and strength through structured survey responses across six organizational dimensions. It produces a quantified culture profile showing the current culture mix and a preferred culture profile showing where employees want the culture to move, enabling gap-based intervention design.

## Purpose
Leaders routinely make decisions about strategy, structure, and people practices without a shared, evidence-based understanding of what their culture actually is. The OCAI provides a standardized, repeatable measure of culture type that transforms qualitative cultural perceptions into a comparable, quantified score set. It enables organizations to have a fact-based conversation about the distance between the current and desired culture, prioritize interventions by quadrant gap, and track cultural evolution over time with a consistent measurement instrument.

## When to Use
- An organization wants a baseline culture measurement before embarking on a strategic transformation or culture change initiative
- The Competing Values Framework has been introduced as the conceptual model and a quantified scoring instrument is needed to operationalize it
- A follow-up culture measurement is needed 12–18 months after a culture intervention to assess progress toward the desired profile
- Business unit or regional comparisons of culture type are required to understand intra-organizational culture variation

## When NOT to Use
- The assessment population is fewer than 20 respondents, making the quadrant averages statistically unreliable
- The organization needs a measure of employee engagement, sentiment, or satisfaction rather than culture type — pulse surveys or engagement surveys are more appropriate
- Leadership is seeking a one-time data point without intent to use the findings to inform interventions — the OCAI generates expectations for change that must be met or trust erodes
- The organization cannot guarantee respondent anonymity, which will suppress honest response and bias results toward socially desirable answers

## Inputs Required
- OCAI survey administered to the target population with confirmed anonymity and a minimum response rate target of 60%
- Organizational segmentation data to enable analysis by level, function, business unit, or geography
- Strategic context document describing the organization's current priorities and the specific performance challenges motivating the culture assessment
- Leadership-defined desired culture profile or, if not yet defined, a facilitated session to produce one using the same OCAI instrument

## Optional Inputs
- Prior OCAI results for longitudinal comparison of culture profile movement over time
- Industry or sector benchmarks for OCAI quadrant scores to contextualize findings against peer organizations
- Qualitative interview data to enrich interpretation of the quantitative quadrant profiles

## Diagnostic Process
1. Administer the OCAI survey to the target population, ensuring that all six organizational dimensions are included: dominant characteristics, organizational leadership, management of employees, organizational glue, strategic emphases, and criteria of success; brief respondents on the forced-choice scoring method (100 points distributed across four options per dimension) and the purpose of the assessment.
2. Collect and aggregate responses across all six dimensions, calculating the average score for each of the four quadrants (Clan, Adhocracy, Market, Hierarchy) per dimension and in total; ensure minimum response thresholds are met before reporting at any sub-group level.
3. Aggregate the quadrant scores across all six dimensions to produce the overall current culture profile; separately aggregate the preferred culture scores to produce the desired culture profile; present both sets of results as radar charts for visual interpretation.
4. Plot the current culture radar chart showing the relative weighting of each quadrant in the organization's current culture profile; annotate with the specific dimension scores contributing to dominant quadrant concentrations.
5. Plot the preferred culture radar chart alongside the current profile to enable direct visual comparison of the gap between where the culture is today and where employees collectively believe it needs to go.
6. Analyze the gap between current and preferred culture by dimension and by quadrant, ranking gaps from largest to smallest; gaps exceeding 8 points in any quadrant at the dimension level are significant and require targeted intervention; identify which specific dimensions are driving each quadrant gap.
7. Recommend targeted interventions per gap area: for Clan gaps, design interventions targeting cross-team collaboration, employee recognition, and people management practices; for Adhocracy gaps, focus on innovation mechanisms, psychological safety, and experimentation norms; for Market gaps, sharpen external accountability structures and performance metrics; for Hierarchy gaps, streamline governance, standardize processes, or clarify role accountabilities depending on direction of the gap.

## Output
The skill produces an OCAI culture report containing the current and preferred culture radar charts, dimension-level quadrant scores, gap analysis ranked by magnitude, narrative interpretation of the dominant culture type and its strategic implications, and a prioritized intervention agenda targeting the largest and most strategically consequential gaps. When run across multiple units, it produces a comparative culture map.
