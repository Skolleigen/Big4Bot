# Competing Values Framework — Example

## Scenario
A traditional regional bank with 1,800 employees has set a strategic objective to become more innovative in product development and customer experience design in response to competitive pressure from digital-first challengers. The Chief People Officer commissions a CVF assessment to diagnose the current culture and identify the magnitude of cultural change required to support the innovation strategy. The survey is administered to 280 employees across all levels and four business units.

## Inputs
- **Organization:** Regional bank, 1,800 employees, four business units (Retail, Commercial, Operations, Technology)
- **Respondents:** 280 employees across all levels; 68% response rate
- **Current CVF profile:** Hierarchy 65%, Market 20%, Clan 10%, Adhocracy 5%
- **Desired CVF profile:** Hierarchy 35%, Market 25%, Clan 20%, Adhocracy 20%
- **Strategic driver:** Innovation mandate in response to digital-first competitor pressure
- **Key leadership concern:** Internal processes are slowing product launches; experimentation is perceived as risky

## Analysis

**Step 1 — CVF survey administration:**
The 24-item CVF instrument is administered via the organization's intranet survey platform over a two-week period. Respondents distribute 100 points across four descriptions for each of six organizational dimensions: dominant characteristics, leadership style, management of employees, organizational glue, strategic emphasis, and criteria of success. Segmentation by level (executive, manager, individual contributor) and business unit is built into the data collection to enable sub-group analysis.

**Step 2 — Current culture profile scoring:**
Aggregated CVF scores across all 280 respondents: Hierarchy = 65%, Market = 20%, Clan = 10%, Adhocracy = 5%. The Hierarchy dominance is consistent across all four business units, though Technology scores slightly lower Hierarchy (58%) and higher Adhocracy (11%) than the overall average. The Operations unit shows the highest Hierarchy concentration at 72%. Individual contributor responses skew more Hierarchy-dominant than executive responses, suggesting that process rigidity is experienced more acutely at frontline levels.

**Step 3 — Desired future culture profile:**
A leadership facilitation session with the 12-member Executive Committee produces the desired culture profile: Hierarchy = 35%, Market = 25%, Clan = 20%, Adhocracy = 20%. The 30-point reduction in Hierarchy is anchored to the strategic goal of accelerating product approval cycles from an average of 14 months to under 6 months. The 15-point Adhocracy increase is linked to the ambition of launching 3 new digital products per year versus the current 1. The Clan increase from 10% to 20% reflects a desire to improve cross-functional collaboration between Technology and the business units.

**Step 4 — Gap calculation:**
Quadrant-level gaps between current and desired profiles: Hierarchy = -30 points (largest reduction required), Adhocracy = +15 points, Clan = +10 points, Market = +5 points. All four quadrants show material gaps exceeding 5 percentage points. The Hierarchy reduction and Adhocracy increase represent the primary transformation required and are structurally linked — practices that currently sustain the Hierarchy culture (multi-layer approval processes, risk aversion norms, low tolerance for failure) directly inhibit the Adhocracy behaviors required (experimentation, rapid prototyping, decentralized decision-making).

**Step 5 — Culture driver and inhibitor identification:**
Primary Hierarchy drivers identified: (a) 7-layer approval process for any product change above $50K threshold, originating from a post-2008 regulatory response; (b) Annual planning cycle with no budget flex for unplanned experiments; (c) Performance management system rewarding error avoidance rather than learning velocity; (d) Leadership role model behaviors — two of the four business unit heads are known to penalize failure publicly. Primary Adhocracy inhibitors: (a) No dedicated innovation time or protected budget for experimentation; (b) No psychological safety mechanism for teams to surface and test ideas below the formal project approval threshold; (c) No cross-functional team structures — all work is organized in siloed functional teams.

**Step 6 — Cultural interventions:**
Six prioritized interventions are recommended: (1) Restructure the product approval process to require only 3 levels of sign-off for initiatives under $500K, reducing approval cycle time by an estimated 60%. (2) Establish a quarterly innovation fund of $2M distributed to business unit innovation pods with a 90-day experiment-and-learn cycle. (3) Redesign performance management criteria to add a "learning and experimentation" dimension weighted at 15% of the overall performance rating. (4) Launch cross-functional product squads pairing Technology with Retail and Commercial business teams, with shared OKRs. (5) Deliver a psychological safety program for all people managers based on Amy Edmondson's framework, making failure-to-learn conversations a structured management practice. (6) Conduct leadership team coaching to shift two specific executive role model behaviors identified as Hierarchy reinforcers.

**Step 7 — Measurement and progress review:**
A follow-up CVF assessment is scheduled for 18 months post-intervention. Interim indicators tracked quarterly: product approval cycle time (target: under 9 months by month 6), number of innovation pod experiments launched (target: 12 in the first year), cross-functional collaboration score from pulse survey (target: 20% improvement from baseline), and performance review completion with the learning dimension (target: 100% inclusion by the next review cycle).
