# Culture Workshops — Example

## Scenario
A private equity-backed consulting firm (500 employees) acquires a technology boutique (200 employees) with a complementary service offering. Within 90 days of closing, integration project teams report persistent friction: consulting firm employees describe the tech firm as "chaotic and disrespectful of process," while tech firm employees describe the consulting firm as "bureaucratic and slow." The Chief Integration Officer commissions three culture workshops across 60 participants drawn from both legacy organizations to surface specific norm conflicts before designing the integrated culture.

## Inputs
- **Organization A:** 500-person consulting firm; culture characterized by hierarchy, client formality, structured delivery methodologies, and partner-led decision-making
- **Organization B:** 200-person technology boutique; culture characterized by autonomy, rapid iteration, flat structure, and engineering-led decisions
- **Workshop participants:** 60 employees across 3 sessions; each session includes 10 participants from each legacy organization, spanning individual contributors, managers, and one senior leader per group
- **Facilitator:** External organizational development consultant with no affiliation to either legacy firm
- **Objective:** Surface and name the specific norm conflicts creating friction; identify which norms from each organization should be preserved in the integrated culture

## Analysis

**Step 1 — Workshop objectives and output definition:**
Three workshop objectives are defined with the Chief Integration Officer: (1) Generate a shared vocabulary for the cultural differences between the two legacy organizations without assigning blame. (2) Identify the specific behavioral norms from each organization that participants want to preserve in the integrated culture. (3) Surface the norm conflicts that are causing the most operational friction and prioritize them for resolution. Target output: a consolidated map of 5–10 norm conflicts with provisional resolution principles, validated across all three workshop sessions.

**Step 2 — Participant selection:**
Each of the three workshops includes 20 participants: 10 from the consulting firm and 10 from the technology boutique, deliberately mixed across teams working on integration projects where friction has been reported. Participants span individual contributors (40%), managers (40%), and one senior leader per session (20%). The selection avoids including direct manager-report pairs to reduce hierarchy effects on candid participation.

**Step 3 — Workshop agenda design:**
Each 4-hour workshop follows the same three-module structure: Module 1 (60 min): Current state norm mapping — participants work in legacy-org pairs to identify the unwritten rules that govern daily behavior in each organization using the prompt "In my organization, the unspoken rule about [topic] is...". Topics include decision-making, client communication, working hours, quality standards, and escalation norms. Module 2 (90 min): Desired state visioning — full group discusses which norms from each organization they would actively choose to keep in a "best of both" integrated culture, and which they would be willing to let go. Module 3 (90 min): Gap and conflict identification — cross-legacy pairs identify the specific norm conflicts causing day-to-day friction and draft resolution principles.

**Step 4 — Facilitated activities:**
Activity 1 — Culture Card Sort: Each participant receives 20 cards describing organizational behaviors (e.g., "decisions require multiple sign-offs," "anyone can challenge any decision in a meeting," "client emails are answered within 1 hour regardless of time zone," "we ship fast and fix later"). Participants sort cards into "describes us," "describes them," and "describes neither." Results reveal strong polarization on 7 cards, confirming material norm distance. Activity 2 — Norm Storytelling: Participants share a specific story where they felt proud of their organization's culture and a story where they were frustrated by it. Cross-organization pairs identify what each story reveals about underlying values. Activity 3 — "Keep, Drop, Build" exercise: Full group categorizes identified norms as "keep from consulting firm," "keep from tech boutique," "drop from both," and "build something new."

**Step 5 — Output documentation and theme synthesis:**
Across the three workshops, 7 recurring norm conflicts are documented and ranked by frequency of mention: (1) Decision authority — consulting firm requires partner sign-off; tech boutique empowers individual engineers. (2) Client communication formality — consulting firm uses structured templated deliverables; tech firm communicates directly and informally. (3) Quality versus speed — consulting firm prioritizes error-free delivery; tech firm prioritizes shipping and iterating. (4) Meeting culture — consulting firm uses structured agendas with pre-reads; tech firm uses ad-hoc stand-ups. (5) Working hours expectations — consulting firm expects extended client-facing hours; tech firm values protected focus time. (6) Escalation norms — consulting firm escalates to senior stakeholders early; tech firm resolves at team level. (7) Recognition — consulting firm recognizes client outcomes; tech firm recognizes technical innovation.

**Step 6 — Prioritization:**
Norm conflicts are assessed on a 2x2 impact-versus-feasibility matrix. Two conflicts are classified as high-impact, high-feasibility and prioritized for resolution first: Decision authority (high friction in integration project teams, addressable by RACI redesign) and quality versus speed (directly affecting joint delivery timelines, addressable by explicit project-level agreements). Three conflicts are classified as high-impact, lower-feasibility and assigned to the culture design workstream: client communication formality, working hours, and recognition. Two conflicts (meeting culture, escalation norms) are classified as lower-impact and assigned to team-level resolution without a centralized mandate.

**Step 7 — Accountability and follow-up:**
Each of the two high-priority conflicts is assigned an Integration Working Group owner and a 60-day resolution deadline. All 60 participants receive a summary of the workshop outputs within five business days. A 30-day pulse check with all participants measures whether the friction signals are reducing. The Chief Integration Officer commits to sharing the integrated culture principles document — informed by workshop outputs — with all 700 employees within 90 days.
