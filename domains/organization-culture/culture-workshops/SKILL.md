# Culture Workshops

## Domain
Organization & Culture

## Description
Facilitated group sessions designed to surface and discuss organizational culture norms, values, and behaviors, generating shared understanding and collective commitment to cultural change. They translate abstract cultural data from surveys and assessments into concrete, owned conversations that build the psychological commitment required for sustained behavior change.

## Purpose
Culture change fails when it is communicated from the top rather than co-created with those who must live it. Culture workshops create structured conditions for employees and leaders to examine existing norms together, name the behaviors that need to shift, and make visible commitments to change that peer accountability can reinforce. They bridge the gap between culture diagnosis (what the data shows) and culture activation (what people actually do differently on Monday morning).

## When to Use
- A culture assessment (CVF, OCAI, or pulse survey) has produced diagnostic data that requires sense-making and collective interpretation before interventions are designed
- A merger, acquisition, or major restructuring has created cultural friction between previously separate employee populations
- Leadership wants to define or refresh a set of organizational values and make them behaviorally specific rather than aspirational slogans
- A specific team or business unit is experiencing persistent interpersonal conflict, collaboration breakdowns, or norm violations that suggest a cultural root cause

## When NOT to Use
- Psychological safety conditions are insufficient for honest participation — workshops in environments with high fear or punitive leadership will produce surface-level outputs rather than genuine cultural insight
- The organization is not prepared to act on workshop outputs; facilitating dialogue that produces no visible follow-through actively damages trust and engagement
- The group size exceeds 80 participants in a single session, making genuine dialogue and co-creation impractical without parallel breakout structures
- The purpose is primarily information-sharing rather than two-way dialogue — town halls and communications are more appropriate for one-way messaging

## Inputs Required
- Culture diagnostic data: survey results, assessment outputs, or leadership observations providing a fact base for workshop discussion
- Defined workshop objectives: what decisions, commitments, or outputs the session is intended to produce
- Participant list with roles and levels represented, ensuring cross-functional and cross-level diversity
- Facilitator with credibility independent of any single stakeholder group

## Optional Inputs
- Prior workshop outputs or previous culture initiative artifacts to avoid repeating past work
- Competitive or external benchmarks showing how peer organizations describe their cultural values
- Ethnographic observations or interview quotes that ground discussion in concrete behavioral examples

## Diagnostic Process
1. Define workshop objectives and target outputs with the commissioning leadership sponsor, distinguishing between exploratory objectives (understand current norms) and generative objectives (define desired behaviors); ensure outputs are decision-relevant and actionable, not merely documentary.
2. Select a representative participant group across organizational levels, functions, geographies, and tenure cohorts; include frontline voices alongside management, as cultural norms are often experienced very differently at different levels.
3. Design the workshop agenda around three core modules: (a) current state mapping — what norms and behaviors actually exist today, (b) desired state visioning — what culture is required to deliver the strategic agenda, (c) gap analysis and commitment — what specifically needs to change and who will hold whom accountable.
4. Facilitate structured activities tailored to the workshop objectives: culture card sorts to surface and prioritize existing norms, storytelling exercises where participants share specific behavioral examples of the culture at its best and worst, norm identification exercises where teams name unwritten rules that govern daily behavior, and appreciative inquiry prompts to identify existing cultural strengths to build on.
5. Document outputs in real time using visible capture methods (flip charts, digital whiteboards) that keep the group aligned on what has been agreed; synthesize themes across breakout groups and surface the two or three most consequential cultural tensions or shifts identified.
6. Prioritize the cultural shifts requiring intervention by applying an impact-versus-feasibility matrix, distinguishing between norm changes that require leadership behavior change (high leverage), systemic changes to processes and incentives (medium leverage), and communication-based awareness shifts (lower leverage but faster to implement).
7. Define specific accountability and follow-up actions before closing the workshop: assign owners to each priority shift, establish a 30-day check-in mechanism, and commit to communicating back to all participants what actions were taken as a result of the session.

## Output
The skill produces a workshop output report containing documented current-state cultural norms, a prioritized list of desired cultural shifts with behavioral specificity, a gap analysis between current and desired norms, and a 30-60-90-day action plan with accountable owners. When delivered across multiple groups, outputs are synthesized into an organizational culture diagnostic that triangulates themes across functions and levels.
