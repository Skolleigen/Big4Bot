# Employee Value Proposition

## Domain
Organization & Culture

## Description
Framework for defining and communicating the total value an organization offers employees in exchange for their skills, capabilities, and contributions, used to attract and retain talent. It articulates the employment deal across dimensions of compensation, development, culture, purpose, and flexibility in a way that is differentiated, authentic, and resonant with target employee segments.

## Purpose
Organizations that cannot articulate why a talented person should choose to work for them — and stay — face structural disadvantages in talent acquisition and retention that compound over time. An EVP provides a clear, evidence-based answer to that question, grounded in employee research rather than leadership aspiration. It enables targeted talent communications, informs HR program investment decisions, and gives leaders a shared vocabulary for describing what the organization offers that goes beyond salary benchmarking.

## When to Use
- Voluntary attrition is elevated relative to sector benchmarks and exit survey data points to a gap between employee expectations and organizational offering
- The organization is preparing to grow headcount significantly and needs a consistent, differentiated employer brand message to support talent acquisition
- Employee engagement scores are declining and pulse survey data suggests dissatisfaction with core elements of the employment deal
- A strategic transformation has materially changed the nature of work or the employee experience and the existing EVP no longer accurately reflects the offering

## When NOT to Use
- The organization is unwilling to make genuine changes to the employment offering — an EVP built on false promises accelerates attrition when employees discover the gap between messaging and reality
- The primary problem is compensation parity, not EVP differentiation — below-market pay must be addressed structurally before EVP work will have impact
- The employee population is too small (fewer than 50 employees) to warrant a formal EVP framework; relationship-based retention is more effective at this scale
- The organization lacks the HR capability or budget to activate and sustain the EVP through consistent recruitment marketing and employee experience programs

## Inputs Required
- Employee research data: engagement survey results, exit interview themes, or focus group findings on what employees value and where the organization falls short
- Voluntary attrition rate by role, level, and tenure versus sector benchmarks
- Current compensation and benefits data benchmarked against the relevant talent market
- Defined target talent segments by role type, career stage, or strategic skill category

## Optional Inputs
- Competitor employer brand analysis showing how peer organizations position their EVP in the talent market
- Employer review platform data (Glassdoor, LinkedIn) synthesizing unsolicited employee sentiment
- Current recruitment conversion data showing where candidates drop out of the hiring process

## Diagnostic Process
1. Segment the workforce by role type (technical, commercial, operational), career stage (early career, mid-career, senior), and strategic talent priority, identifying which segments are most critical to retain and which require differentiated EVP messaging.
2. Conduct employee research across each priority segment using a combination of quantitative surveys (what matters most in your work experience, rated by importance and satisfaction) and qualitative interviews (what made you stay, what has frustrated you, what would make you leave); synthesize findings into a ranked importance-satisfaction matrix.
3. Benchmark current EVP elements against the relevant talent market across five dimensions: compensation and benefits (total cash, equity, benefits), career development (growth pathways, learning investment, internal mobility), culture and environment (leadership quality, team dynamics, inclusion), purpose and mission (organizational impact, values alignment, work meaningfulness), and flexibility (remote work, schedule autonomy, workload sustainability).
4. Identify the gaps between the current organizational offering and employee expectations by dimension, distinguishing between hygiene factors (threshold requirements where a gap causes attrition) and motivator factors (differentiators where strength drives attraction and engagement).
5. Define the EVP pillars — typically 3 to 5 themes — that honestly represent the strongest and most differentiated elements of the employment offer; anchor each pillar to specific programs, policies, and employee experiences that substantiate the claim, not aspirational statements without grounding.
6. Validate the draft EVP with employee focus groups drawn from each target segment, testing message resonance, authenticity, and clarity; revise pillars based on feedback before finalizing.
7. Develop an activation and communication plan covering internal EVP communication to current employees (reinforcing and retaining), external employer brand expression through recruitment channels and social media, and manager enablement so that hiring managers can articulate the EVP conversationally during interviews and onboarding.

## Output
The skill produces an EVP document defining 3–5 validated pillars with supporting proof points for each, a gap analysis showing where the current offering falls short of employee expectations by segment, and an activation roadmap covering internal communication, employer brand expression, and HR program investment priorities. When delivered with attrition analysis, it includes a cost-of-attrition estimate and projected retention ROI from specific EVP investments.
