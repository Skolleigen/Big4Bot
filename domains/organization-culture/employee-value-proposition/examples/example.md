# Employee Value Proposition — Example

## Scenario
A 600-person fintech company growing rapidly in the payments infrastructure sector is experiencing 28% annual voluntary attrition against a sector median of 14%. The People Analytics team has identified that the problem is concentrated in two segments: mid-career software engineers (3–7 years tenure) and product managers. Exit surveys from the prior 12 months reveal "limited career development" as the most cited reason for departure (44% of responses) and "below-market compensation" as the second (31%). The Chief People Officer initiates a full EVP redesign to arrest attrition and strengthen the employer brand ahead of a planned headcount increase of 150 engineers in the next 18 months.

## Inputs
- **Organization:** 600-person fintech company, payments infrastructure
- **Voluntary attrition rate:** 28% (sector median: 14%); concentrated in software engineers and product managers
- **Exit survey themes:** Limited career development (44%), below-market compensation (31%), lack of flexibility (12%), culture concerns (9%), other (4%)
- **Priority segments:** Mid-career software engineers (3–7 years tenure), product managers (all levels)
- **Strategic context:** 150-engineer headcount growth planned in next 18 months; employer brand investment required
- **Current EVP:** No formal EVP document; employer brand messaging focuses on "fast-paced fintech" and "impact at scale"

## Analysis

**Step 1 — Workforce segmentation:**
Three priority segments are defined based on strategic importance and attrition concentration: (1) Mid-career software engineers — highest attrition rate (34%), highest replacement cost (estimated $85K per departure including recruiting and productivity ramp), and most critical to the 18-month growth plan. (2) Product managers — second highest attrition (26%) and a strategic talent shortage in the fintech market making backfill increasingly difficult. (3) Early-career graduates — lower attrition (14%) but volume hiring segment where employer brand strength determines application quality and volume. EVP design will address all three but with segment-specific emphasis on the first two.

**Step 2 — Employee research:**
A mixed-method research program is conducted across 90 employees. Quantitative: 72-item survey administered to 350 employees (62% response rate) measuring importance and satisfaction across 24 EVP attributes. Qualitative: 30-minute interviews with 40 employees across priority segments including both high performers who have stayed and employees currently job-searching (identified through voluntary disclosure in exit prediction risk modeling). Key survey findings: "Career growth pathways" scores 4.6/5 on importance but only 2.1/5 on satisfaction — the largest importance-satisfaction gap in the data. "Compensation fairness" scores 4.5 importance and 2.6 satisfaction. "Flexibility" scores 4.2 importance and 3.1 satisfaction — notable gap but not as severe. "Mission and impact" scores 3.9 importance and 4.1 satisfaction — a genuine strength. Qualitative themes: Engineers cite that internal promotion criteria are opaque, lateral moves between domains are not actively enabled, and learning budget exists on paper but accessing it requires manager approval that is frequently slow or denied in practice.

**Step 3 — Market benchmarking:**
Benchmarking across 12 peer fintech and technology companies across five dimensions: (a) Total compensation — the company's base salary percentiles are P50 for engineers and P45 for product managers, with equity at P40 relative to peer set; the compensation gap is real and significant enough to require structural adjustment before EVP messaging can credibly address it. (b) Career development — 8 of 12 peers have formal internal career frameworks with published leveling criteria; the company has internal grades but no published criteria. (c) Culture — Glassdoor score of 3.4 versus peer median of 3.8; leadership ratings are the lowest-scoring category on reviews. (d) Purpose and mission — the payments infrastructure mission scores well against peers in employee research; this is a genuine differentiator in a market where many peers have less clear societal impact narratives. (e) Flexibility — the company's hybrid policy (3 days in office) is less flexible than 7 of 12 peers who have moved to 2-day or fully flexible arrangements.

**Step 4 — Gap identification:**
Four material EVP gaps are identified: (1) Career development (hygiene-level gap) — no published career framework, opaque promotion criteria, and friction in accessing learning budget create a perception that growth is accidental rather than structured. (2) Compensation equity (hygiene-level gap) — P45 for product managers and P40 equity for engineers are below the threshold required for competitive talent retention in the current market. (3) Flexibility (motivator gap) — 3-day office policy is disadvantageous relative to peers and is mentioned in 40% of Glassdoor reviews as a friction point. (4) Leadership quality (motivator gap) — leadership ratings on both internal survey and Glassdoor are the lowest-scoring dimension; manager capability is a systemic issue requiring investment independent of EVP messaging.

**Step 5 — EVP pillar definition:**
Four EVP pillars are defined, each grounded in specific programs and policies: (1) Grow Your Career — anchored by a new published Engineering Career Framework launching in Q2 with transparent leveling criteria, a formalized internal mobility program enabling lateral moves, and a refreshed $3,000 annual learning budget with no manager-approval friction below $500. (2) Rewarded Fairly — anchored by a compensation equity review moving engineers to P65 base and P55 equity within 12 months, and a twice-annual compensation review cycle replacing the once-annual process. (3) Work That Matters — anchored by the genuine payments infrastructure mission, customer impact storytelling program, and participation in two industry working groups on financial inclusion. (4) Built for Flexibility — anchored by a revised 2-day office policy launching in Q3 and a formal "results, not hours" manager training program.

**Step 6 — Focus group validation:**
Draft EVP pillars are tested with three focus groups of 12 employees each, drawn from the two priority segments and one early-career cohort. Pillar 1 (Grow Your Career) receives the strongest resonance: 91% rate it as "highly credible if the career framework launches as described." Pillar 2 (Rewarded Fairly) receives strong resonance on the compensation review commitment (86% credible) but moderate resonance on the twice-annual cycle (63% credible — employees are skeptical it will be executed consistently). Pillar 3 (Work That Matters) is credible for engineers and less so for product managers, who feel less connected to the mission in their day-to-day work. Pillar 4 (Built for Flexibility) is the strongest immediate pull factor for mid-career engineers, with 78% saying it would influence their decision to accept an offer.

**Step 7 — Activation and communication plan:**
Internal activation: CEO town hall announcing the EVP and specific program commitments in month 1. People manager training on how to articulate the EVP in 1:1 conversations. Internal careers page redesign. External activation: LinkedIn employer brand content series featuring 12 employee stories aligned to each pillar. Engineering blog relaunched with technical content to build credibility with target engineers. Glassdoor response program addressing the most common review themes directly. Recruitment team training so hiring managers can deliver the EVP narrative consistently during interviews.
