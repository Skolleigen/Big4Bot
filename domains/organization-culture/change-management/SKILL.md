# Change Management

## Domain
Organization & Culture

## Description
Structured framework for planning and executing organizational change, managing stakeholder resistance, and sustaining adoption of new ways of working. It translates a business transformation initiative into a sequenced set of people-side interventions covering communication, capability building, and resistance mitigation.

## Purpose
Large-scale organizational changes — system migrations, restructurings, mergers, and operating model shifts — fail primarily due to people factors, not technical ones. Change management provides a structured methodology to maximize employee adoption and minimize productivity loss during transitions. It gives leaders a replicable process for moving stakeholders from awareness through commitment, ensuring business case benefits are realized rather than eroded by poor adoption.

## When to Use
- An organization is implementing a significant technology, process, or structural change affecting 50 or more employees
- Leadership has defined a business case for transformation but lacks a structured approach to the people-side of implementation
- Prior change initiatives at the organization have stalled due to resistance, low adoption, or communication failures
- A readiness assessment, pulse survey, or leadership signal suggests high levels of anxiety or uncertainty among affected populations

## When NOT to Use
- The change is minor, reversible, or affects fewer than 10 people and does not alter core workflows or reporting relationships
- The organization is in active crisis and requires immediate stabilization before a structured change process can be designed
- Sponsorship from senior leadership is absent — change management cannot succeed without visible, committed executive sponsorship
- The change scope, business case, or end-state is not yet defined; change management requires a clear destination to plan toward

## Inputs Required
- Defined change scope: description of what is changing, why, and what the future state looks like
- Stakeholder inventory: list of affected populations segmented by role, location, or function
- Timeline and key milestones for the change initiative
- Organizational readiness data: baseline survey or interview findings on employee confidence and awareness

## Optional Inputs
- Prior change history at the organization and lessons learned from previous initiatives
- Existing communication channels and their reach and credibility with affected populations
- HR or line manager capacity available to support the change effort

## Diagnostic Process
1. Define the change scope and business case by clarifying what specifically is changing, who is affected, the rationale, and the measurable outcomes the change is intended to deliver; ensure alignment between project leadership and the change management workstream on scope boundaries.
2. Assess organizational readiness using a structured survey or facilitated interviews covering employee awareness of the change, confidence in the new way of working, trust in leadership, and perceived impact on day-to-day work; score results by population segment and identify lowest-readiness groups.
3. Identify and segment stakeholders by impact (degree to which their work changes) and influence (degree to which they shape others' adoption); classify each segment as champion, neutral, skeptic, or blocker and prioritize engagement accordingly.
4. Develop a communication plan per stakeholder group specifying message content tailored to each group's primary concerns, channel selection (town halls, manager cascade, intranet, team meetings), frequency, and accountable sender; ensure frontline managers receive messages before their teams.
5. Design a training and capability-building program matched to the new skills and behaviors required; distinguish between awareness-level training for all employees, role-specific procedural training for directly impacted workers, and advanced capability-building for change champions and managers.
6. Identify sources of resistance and develop targeted mitigation tactics for each: address job security fears with transparent communications about role impacts, address complexity concerns with role-specific job aids and peer coaching, and address trust gaps by increasing leadership visibility and two-way dialogue mechanisms.
7. Monitor adoption metrics on a biweekly cadence — system usage rates, process compliance, training completion, and employee sentiment from pulse surveys — and adjust communication, training, or support tactics for population segments falling below adoption targets.

## Output
The skill produces a change management plan document covering stakeholder segmentation, communication plan, training program design, resistance mitigation tactics, and a monitoring dashboard with adoption KPIs by population segment. When delivered for a specific initiative, it also includes a 90-day action calendar and accountability matrix.
