# Competitive Positioning

## Domain
Market & Customer

## Description
Framework for defining how a product or organization occupies a distinct and valuable position in the minds of target customers relative to competitors. Produces an explicit positioning statement and supporting evidence that guides messaging, product development, and sales strategy.

## Purpose
Without deliberate positioning, customers default to commodity comparisons that compress margins and increase churn. This framework surfaces the competitive landscape, identifies where meaningful differentiation exists or can be created, and translates that differentiation into a defensible market position. The output resolves ambiguity in how the organization should present itself and prioritize capabilities relative to the competition.

## When to Use
- A company is launching a new product or entering a market where established competitors already operate
- Customer research or lost-deal analysis indicates that the current value proposition is not resonating or is perceived as undifferentiated
- A competitor has made a significant move (product launch, acquisition, price reduction) that requires a strategic positioning response
- A rebranding or go-to-market redesign requires an anchoring positioning statement

## When NOT to Use
- The market is so nascent that no meaningful competitive set exists and category creation, not positioning, is the primary challenge
- Organizational leadership has not aligned on the target customer segment, making positioning premature
- The company lacks the resources or timeline to act on positioning changes identified by the analysis

## Inputs Required
- List of direct and indirect competitors operating in the target market
- Defined target customer segment(s) with articulated buying criteria
- Own product or service feature set and known customer perception data
- Pricing data for key competitors

## Optional Inputs
- Win/loss analysis data from the sales function
- Customer or prospect survey data on brand perception and competitive preference

## Diagnostic Process
1. Identify direct competitors (same product category, same target customer) and indirect competitors (alternative solutions that solve the same customer problem through different means). Document each competitor's primary product offering, target segment, and publicly stated value proposition.
2. Define the competitive dimensions most relevant to target customers. Dimensions typically include product capabilities, price, brand trust, service quality, speed of deployment, and integration ecosystem. Validate dimensions against customer buying criteria data rather than internal assumptions.
3. Map all competitors on a two-dimensional positioning matrix using the two dimensions that best discriminate among players in the market. Plot own position alongside competitors to visualize the current competitive landscape.
4. Identify white space on the positioning matrix where no competitor currently operates and where target customer demand exists. Assess whether white space represents a genuine unmet need or a gap that competitors have rationally chosen to avoid.
5. Evaluate the organization's current positioning: assess whether it is clearly communicated, whether customers perceive it as claimed, and whether it is differentiated enough to influence purchase decisions. Use win/loss data and customer verbatims where available.
6. Define or refine a positioning statement using the standard structure: For [target segment], [brand/product] is the [category] that [primary differentiation] because [proof point]. The statement must be specific, testable, and defensible over a 12-24 month horizon.
7. Test the positioning statement against customer perception by validating with a sample of target customers or internal customer-facing teams. Assess whether the differentiation claimed is credible, relevant, and distinct from what competitors claim.

## Output
The analysis produces a competitive positioning matrix showing the organization relative to key competitors, a validated positioning statement, an assessment of positioning strengths and vulnerabilities, and prioritized recommendations for reinforcing or shifting the position. Outputs are structured to directly inform messaging frameworks, sales enablement, and product roadmap prioritization.

## References
- Al Ries & Jack Trout: *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (1981) — Foundational practitioner work establishing positioning as a customer-perception discipline rather than a product attribute exercise
- Michael E. Porter: *Competitive Strategy* (1980) — Provides the structural industry analysis (Five Forces) that contextualizes competitive positioning choices
- Michael Treacy & Fred Wiersema: *The Discipline of Market Leaders* (1995) — Defines the three value disciplines (operational excellence, product leadership, customer intimacy) as mutually exclusive positioning archetypes
